## 1. Outer Binary — Quantized Energy Shifts, Nonlocal Inputs

### Reality (What happens)
- The outer binary shifts its energy in *quantized* increments of $\hbar$, meaning an external architrino interaction either deposits an energy quantum or doesn’t—no partial measures.
- Crucially, the outcome is not purely determined by local conditions: it depends on potential contributions from **architrinos outside the local causal cone**—meaning from the perspective of the outer binary, it’s being perturbed by field states that originate in regions not classically “in contact.”
- The "quantized selection" is non-deterministic only from the local perspective; it still arises from a deterministic global architrino state.

### Causality
- Globally: still deterministic, since all architrinos transmit potential via path histories in absolute time, and the evolving state depends on the whole assembly configuration.
- Locally: looks nonlocal or stochastic, because inputs are coming from sources possibly with $v > c_f$ or from prior self-hit reflections—so from the subsystem’s local light cone perspective, the triggers are "untraceable."
- This produces an *effective* causality violation for observers that only see part of the world, even though on the total architrino ledger nothing actually breaks.

### Randomness / Chaos / Free Will
- Randomness is emergent from *state ignorance.* Since not all architrinos are trackable operationally, transitions appear probabilistic.
- Chaos is plausible, because small differences in remote or high-$v$ inputs could tip the quantized decision. However, it’s deterministic chaos, not fundamental randomness.
- Free will: in this framework, what we call "choices" in an outer-level system (like a neuron) could trace back to tiny, externally-seeded, quantized energy shifts. So "freedom" would come from untrackable global inputs, not uncaused self-action.

## 2. Middle Binary — Symmetry-Breaking Multiplier

### Reality (What happens)
- This binary sits on the razor’s edge: velocity exactly $c_f$. A minuscule perturbation pushes it into $v<c_f$ or $v>c_f$ territory.
- Yet, unlike the outer binary, the energy change isn’t a yes/no $\hbar$ transfer—it’s a **multiplicative response:** e.g. a single $\hbar$ input can lock into $2\hbar$ output if the system tips into a self-hit amplification mode.
- This means the middle binary acts as the “leverage point” that decides whether a micro input stays micro or cascades upward.

### Causality
- Again, globally deterministic. But the middle binary is what transduces external potentials into either stable behavior or runaway self-hit dynamics.
- Because it sits at the $v=c_f$ threshold, the slightest nonlocal influence can rapidly change the entire tri-binary’s state, making it the propagator of apparent randomness.

### Randomness / Chaos / Free Will
- Random-seeming “choices” aren’t truly random; they are the result of the middle binary’s sensitivity to minute perturbations from the rest of the Architrino sea.
- Chaos: this is the true dynamical source. Small external inputs can be amplified by the middle binary, leading to macroscopic shifts. So chaotic behavior is expected—and is mechanistically traceable to the symmetry-breaking role of the mid binary.
- Free will: if consciousness or decision-making aligns with middle-binary logic, then “free will” is the emergent perception of the internal assembly responding to subtle external biases. The system is deterministic, but because the determining influences are from a vast, generally untrackable environment (including $v>c_f$ actors), the outcomes are effectively unpredictable.

### Takeaway
- Reality in this hypothetical regime is entirely deterministic at the Architrino level but operationally nonlocal and chaotic at the assembly level.
- Causality is globally preserved; any apparent violation is due to partial information and the finite field-speed branching of potentials.
- Randomness or free will are emergent illusions: the system is highly sensitive to phase transitions at the middle binary (symmetry point), and transitions in the outer binary are triggered by inputs from vast, effectively unknowable architrino networks.
